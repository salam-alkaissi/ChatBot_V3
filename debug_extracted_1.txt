‚Ä¢ A freedom of the individual from interference or intrusion, the right ‚Äòto be left alone‚Äô

‚Ä¢ intrusion into private affairs;

Principles of the GDPR (Summary)
According to the GDPR, the obligations of data processors and controllers:
‚Ä¢ Lawful, fair, and transparent processing. The burden of proof is on the data controller and processor.  
Access to personal data is based on consent, legal obligation, the subject‚Äôs vital interest, public interest, etc.
‚Ä¢ Purpose limitation: collected for specified, explicit and legitimate purposes and not further processed in a 
manner that is incompatible with those purposes.
‚Ä¢ Data minimisation: limited to what is necessary in relation to the purposes for which they are processed.
‚Ä¢ Accuracy: accurate and, where necessary, kept up to date.
‚Ä¢ Storage limitation: kept in a form which permits identification of data subjects for no longer than is 
necessary for the purposes for which the personal data are processed.
‚Ä¢ Integrity and confidentiality: ensures appropriate security of the personal data, including protection against 
unauthorised or unlawful processing and against accidental loss, destruction or damage.

De-Identification, Anonymisation, Pseudonymisation
‚Ä¢ De-identification of a dataset: removal of direct identifiers.
‚Ä¢ Anonymisation of a dataset: removal of all direct and indirect identifiers.
‚Ä¢ Re-identification or deanonymisation: finding out to whom a previously de-identified
or anonymised dataset refers (which is what we want to avoid).
‚Ä¢ Pseudonymisation of a dataset: replace identifiers by single-use, meaningless pseudonyms
(e.g. unique random numbers). Using a lookup table maintained by a trusted third party,
re-identification becomes possible (ex.: life-threatening disease discovered in research).
‚Ä¢ Full anonymisation can be very hard. There are various techniques,
but there is no general recipe, as the success depends on the data and the context.
‚Ä¢ Example: is age alone:
‚Ä¢ a direct identifier? No.
‚Ä¢ an indirect identifier? No.
‚Ä¢ But it can be, depending on the context: if the number of subjects is low,
or if the age is extreme (110 years).
‚Ä¢ Sweeney (2002) found that 87% of the population in the United States can be uniquely 
identified with three indirect identifiers: gender, date of birth, and five-digit zip code.

Domain-Specific Named Entity Recognition
‚Ä¢ NER is typically a domain-specific task. Depending on the domain, the entity types
to be recognised will be different.
‚Ä¢ Texts show extreme variations in length, orthography, lexicon, grammar, register, and style.
‚Ä¢ Medical records:
‚Ä¢ medium-length (up to a page);
‚Ä¢ specialised medical language;
‚Ä¢ person names, IDs, institutions, age, date, disease names, procedure names, etc.;
‚Ä¢ pronouns and gender-specific morphemes would rather be found via part-of-speech tagging.
‚Ä¢ Legal documents:
‚Ä¢ long pieces of text (several pages);
‚Ä¢ specialised legal language;
‚Ä¢ much more open-ended in terms of direct and indirect identifiers.
‚Ä¢ Tweets:
‚Ä¢ very short pieces of text;
‚Ä¢ non-standard orthography and grammar, #hashtags, @identifiers, acronyms, typos, etc.;
‚Ä¢ anything could theoretically appear.
‚Ä¢ The NER model needs to be built/trained/finetuned to the domain and task.
Reuse across domains and tasks is partial at best.

Definitions: Harm
‚Ä¢ It is always implied that unfairness has negative consequences on individuals
and social groups, which we call harm.
‚Ä¢ Bias causes systematic harm.
‚Ä¢ Allocational harm: resources are distributed unevenly or unfairly.
Examples:
‚Ä¢ you do not get a job because you were misclassified as less apt because of your name or gender;
‚Ä¢ a bank does not give you credit because its trained algorithm discriminates against certain
addresses or names;
‚Ä¢ the state classifies you as an abuser of unemployment benefit because of your lower social status.
‚Ä¢ Representational harm: social groups or individuals are stigmatised or stereotyped.
Examples:
‚Ä¢ a machine translator makes gender assumptions based on profession;
‚Ä¢ certain races are underrepresented in media, or only in stereotypical ways.

Lessons Learnt and Open Questions about Bias
Ethics in NLP
‚Ä¢ There is no consensus of what constitutes bias, discrimination, hate speech, etc.,
and this is not likely to change.
‚Ä¢ Certain types of bias have been widely covered (gender, race), others much less
(languages, dialects, social groups).
‚Ä¢ Identification of bias has been rudimentary (based on predefined word lists, concepts,
or the LM‚Äôs own judgment).
‚Ä¢ Risk of low precision: overzealous debiasing leading to certain words
disappearing from the LM‚Äôs output, independently of meaning or context.
‚Ä¢ Risk of low recall: more subtle forms of discrimination are not detected.
‚Ä¢ Engineers need to commit to an ethical stance and work according to it right from design.
‚Ä¢ The ethical stance and the features built into systems that follow from it
(e.g. taboo words, filters, etc.) must be made explicit.
